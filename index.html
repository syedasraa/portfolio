01
Product Discovery · UX Research · B2C Platform

PawsForward — Animal Shelter Platform

Connecting hearts with shelter animals through transparent, emotionally-driven product design.

Problem Statement

Animal shelters in Germany operate at 69% overcapacity (German Animal Welfare Federation), yet willing donors disengage due to a lack of transparency, trust, and meaningful ongoing connection. There was no platform solving both sides of this equation.

My Role & Research Approach
Role
Documentation Lead · Research contributor · Presentation owner
Duration
4 months · Team of 3
Research Methods
Semi-structured interviews, 5-Whys root cause analysis, Five W's framework, competitor desk research
Validation
10-participant usability test (remote + in-person), SUS scoring, time-on-task
Key Product Insights
47% of non-adopters cited lack of time as the main barrier — the product needed to make support frictionless, not time-intensive.
Trust, not empathy, was the real blocker. Users wanted to help but distrusted how money was used. Transparent donation tracking became a non-negotiable feature, not a nice-to-have.
Engagement drops without feedback loops. Users needed to see the impact of their contribution — a virtual pet mechanic and monthly summaries were designed as retention tools, not cosmetic features.
Feature Prioritisation (PM View)

After user testing, I reframed the improvement backlog using impact vs. effort logic:

High Impact · Low Effort — Build First
Guest checkout (no forced signup for one-time donation). Sorting/filtering by urgency and goal progress.
High Impact · High Effort — Plan Next
Shelter backend dashboard. Subscription management (pause/cancel). Social sharing with impact data.
User Testing Outcomes
Emotional design resonance
92%
Sponsorship model clarity
88%
Navigation intuitiveness
85%
Payment flow success rate
100%

PM Reflection: If I were a working PM on this product, the single highest-priority fix would be removing forced account creation for one-time donations. Every participant flagged it as a deal-breaker — it has direct revenue impact and a clear solution (guest checkout flow with optional account creation post-payment).

Sprint Week — Applied Research Project

A cross-functional product sprint: from problem definition to tested prototype in 5 business days.

Context

A structured 5-day Agile sprint simulating a real product delivery cycle. The team operated full Scrum — daily stand-ups, sprint planning, a mid-week review, and a final retrospective. I took ownership of documentation, sprint coordination, and the final presentation deliverable.

How I Operated in Agile
Sprint Planning
Defined user stories with acceptance criteria. Scoped the sprint to what was achievable in 5 days — consciously cut 2 features to protect delivery.
Daily Stand-ups
Facilitated daily 15-min syncs: what's done, what's blocked, what's next. Tracked blockers and resolved two cross-team dependencies.
Documentation Lead
Owned sprint notes, decision log, and the final project report. Wrote the retrospective findings into a structured summary for the teaching team.
Retrospective
Facilitated a team retrospective. Key learnings: earlier user testing reduces late rework; clearer acceptance criteria shortens review cycles.

Midway through the sprint, the team's scope was at risk of overrunning. Interaction Design · Accessibility · Requirements Definition

AID Hilfe — Accessible Interaction Design

Identifying and solving an accessibility gap through structured research and validated design.

Problem

Existing digital support tools failed to serve users with specific accessibility needs. The project aimed to identify the precise gap through research, define product requirements for an inclusive solution, and validate the design with the target user group.

PM-Style Problem Definition
Who is affected
Users requiring accessible digital interfaces — identified through contextual research and user interviews.
Business impact
Accessibility gaps exclude a significant user segment and create compliance risk for digital products in EU markets.
Solution constraint
Solution had to work without additional hardware. Software-only, retrofittable to existing products.
Success metric
Target users can complete core tasks independently, without support from another person.
Key Design Trade-offs Made
Simplicity over features: Chose a focused single-function interaction over a multi-mode system. The more options, the more cognitive load for the target user.
Validation over assumption: Iterated twice based on user feedback rather than shipping the first concept. The second iteration had significantly higher task success rates.

What this demonstrates for a PM role: I can translate a user research finding into a product requirement with clear acceptance criteria. I don't just note that something is hard to use — I define what "fixed" looks like, and how to measure it.

04
Inclusive Design · Hardware Prototype · Product Spec

Touchless Smart Bin — Inclusive Hardware Design

Defining product requirements for a sensor-based accessibility device for wheelchair users and the elderly.

Product Specification (PM Format)
Problem
Standard waste bins require physical contact or bending — both inaccessible for wheelchair users and elderly individuals.
Target users
Wheelchair users, elderly individuals with limited mobility. Secondary: any user in hygiene-critical environments.
Solution
Ultrasonic sensor detects motion at proximity. Lid opens within 0.1 seconds. Fully contactless and hands-free.
Success criteria
Wheelchair user can use bin independently. Response time under 0.2s. No physical contact required at any step.
What I Would Add as a PM
Market sizing: 1.8M wheelchair users in Germany; accessible public infrastructure is a growing regulatory mandate under the EU Accessibility Act 2025.
Competitive landscape: Existing automatic bins are primarily foot-pedal or sensor-on-top — both inaccessible for wheelchair height. This is an unaddressed gap.
Next iteration priority: Battery life and maintenance cycle — the biggest operational concern for institutional buyers (hospitals, care homes).

This project taught me to think beyond the prototype — a product that works in a lab must also work in an institution. The real product questions are about deployment, maintenance, and procurement, not just the sensor response time.
