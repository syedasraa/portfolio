About Me

The person behind
the projects

I'm a master's student in Usability Engineering at Hochschule Rhein-Waal in Germany, with a bachelor's in Computer Science and Engineering. My undergraduate capstone — a solar-powered water solutions system combining IoT, machine learning, and a Django backend — won the Best Final Year Project award. That experience shaped how I think about design: systems don't work unless the people who depend on them can actually use them.

Moving to Germany for my master's shifted my focus from building systems to understanding the humans inside them. Since then I've worked across emergency healthcare apps, animal welfare platforms, a sprint-week logistics tool, and a digital fabrication project for inclusive design — each time trying to hold the research and the design together rather than treating them as separate phases.

I work comfortably in Figma across the full fidelity range, from rough wireframes to interactive hi-fi prototypes. My research side includes user interviews, competitive analysis, usability testing, and persona work. My CS background means I can have technical conversations with developers and understand the constraints that shape what gets built.

Case Study 01

AID Hilfe — Emergency UX

Applied Interaction Design, SS2025 · Team: Andrea Polk, Dharshini Punniyamurthy, Hafsa Ashfaque, Jiya, Sumaira Hedaoo, Syeda Asra Anjum · Tools: Figma, FigJam, Miro · March – May 2025

1
The Problem

International students in Germany face three compounding problems during medical emergencies: they cannot speak German to call for help, they don't know the difference between 112 and the out-of-hours GP service, and they don't know which hospitals will accept their insurance. Each problem on its own is manageable — all three together, under panic, is not.

The project goal was to design a mobile app that removes those barriers: a way to call an ambulance without speaking German, report symptoms through voice, text or images, and see which hospitals nearby accept the user's insurance.

"How do you help a panicked student, far from home, get urgent medical help in a country where they can't speak the language or navigate the system?"

2
Research

User personas were built to represent the range of international students using the app — varying by language background, digital literacy, and the type of emergency they might face. The personas helped the team stay focused on emotional state (panic, confusion, shame) rather than just functional requirements.

Competitive analysis covered existing emergency and health navigation apps, including Where Are U, to identify what was already available and where the gaps were. The analysis found no existing solution addressed language barriers, system literacy, and insurance routing together in a single flow.

Desk research informed the overall problem scope and fed directly into the feature prioritisation decisions.

3
Design Process

The team moved through moodboarding and brand identity work before wireframing, which helped establish a tone for the product — calm, clear, and directive, rather than clinical or alarming. The visual language was intentionally drawn from hospital wayfinding systems and instructional kits: spaces designed to guide people under stress.

Typography: Montserrat for headings (geometric, approachable) paired with Noto Sans for body text — chosen for its multilingual character support and high legibility at small sizes, directly relevant for an international audience.

Mid-fidelity prototypes were built and tested before the team moved to high-fidelity screens in Figma. The design system was documented as part of the deliverables.

4
Usability Testing

Usability testing was conducted with a group of international students using the mid-fidelity prototype. The sessions surfaced three specific issues:

— The emergency button did not feel prominent enough for a moment where users would be under stress and looking for immediate affordance.

— Blinking UI indicators created confusion rather than urgency.

— The number of steps in the core emergency flow was too high for a critical-path action.

All three findings directly changed the design: the emergency button was enlarged and repositioned, the blinking was removed in favour of solid colour coding, and the emergency call flow was reduced to the minimum viable steps.

The project set a success target of a SUS score of 80 or above, reflecting the standard for high user satisfaction, and a user confidence rate of 70% or more — meaning at least 70% of participants should report feeling more prepared to handle a medical emergency after using the app.

5
Outcome & Reflection

The project produced a fully documented Figma hi-fi prototype covering the ambulance call, symptom reporting, and hospital finder flows, alongside the design system and a prototype walkthrough.

The team noted that future testing should include stress simulation — participants in a calm lab setting behave differently than someone actually facing a medical emergency. Testing under simulated time pressure would likely reveal friction the current sessions did not surface.

Expanding the participant pool to include a more demographically diverse group of international students would also catch cultural nuances the current prototype may not account for.

Case Study 02

PawsForward — Social Impact Platform

ARP A · High Fidelity Presentation · Team: Muhammet Necip Sagar (UX Research Lead), Syeda Musarat Jahan Ramisa (Design Lead), Syeda Asra Anjum (Documentation Lead) · Tools: Figma, Miro

1
The Problem

German animal shelters are overcrowded — 69% are at high capacity according to the German Animal Welfare Federation — while 44% of German households have at least one pet (ZZF). The gap is not a shortage of people willing to help; it's a breakdown in the connection between willing supporters and shelters that need them.

Desk research using DA-direkt data identified the top reasons people don't adopt: 47% cite lack of time, 29% insufficient living space, 17% cost. This shaped the design direction away from adoption-only and toward multiple forms of support — one-time donations, monthly sponsorship, and wishlist-based giving — so people without the capacity to adopt could still contribute meaningfully.

Problem statement: Animal shelters struggle with overcrowding and inconsistent support, while willing donors disengage due to a lack of transparency, trust, and meaningful ongoing connection — leaving shelters without reliable, long-term support systems.

2
Research

Semi-structured interviews were conducted to understand user attitudes toward helping shelter animals, identify barriers such as lack of time, trust, or awareness, and explore what emotional triggers motivate action — empathy, transparency, visible impact.

Root cause analysis (5 Whys) connected surface-level user behaviour to the underlying design opportunities. Rather than designing for symptoms ("people don't donate"), the method pointed toward the cause ("people don't trust where money goes" and "people feel disconnected from the outcome").

Five W's and H framing explored the problem from multiple perspectives — who experiences it, when and where the challenges occur, and what users currently do instead.

Three detailed user personas were developed — a shelter manager (Anna, 41, Hamburg), a student supporter (Amina, 24, Duisburg), and a young professional donor (Lukas, 29, Berlin) — each with full motivation, needs, goals, pain points, wants, and fears.

Three customer journey maps were produced, one per persona, tracing six stages: Awareness → Research → Decision → Onboarding → Ongoing Engagement → Reflection. Each stage included user activities, goals, obstacles, and design opportunities.

3
Design & Features

Animal profiles were built around storytelling rather than data — photos, background stories, health status, personality traits, and real-time updates from caretakers. The framing emerged from research: users respond to animals as individuals, not statistics.

Sponsorship tiers (€5–€50) were kept simple and graduated, with concrete impact descriptions for each level — e.g., "Feeds Charlie for 1 day." This addresses the transparency finding directly.

Impact dashboard shows a clear breakdown of where money goes — medical care, food, enrichment, training — visible before and after payment. This feature was the direct design response to the trust deficit identified in interviews.

A SWOT analysis identified the platform's strengths (engaging animal profiles, transparent donation ecosystem, dual adoption/sponsorship pathways), weaknesses (information overload risk, dependence on accurate shelter data), opportunities (growing public interest in animal welfare, gamification), and threats (competition, regulatory challenges, trust and credibility risks).

4
Usability Testing

Testing used two methods: remote sessions over Google Meet with screen sharing and think-aloud protocol, and in-person sessions where the System Usability Scale was administered after task completion and time-on-task was recorded.

10 participants: 4 friends and family members, 2 university students, 2 employees from animal shelters, 1 UX designer, 1 app developer. Tasks included browsing animal profiles and stories, sponsoring an animal, and exploring features.

What worked: Emotional design resonated immediately. The sponsorship model and donation tiers were understood without explanation. Navigation was intuitive on first use. The payment flow from animal selection to payment page was clear and logical.

Issues found: No way to cancel or pause monthly subscriptions — unclear exit path. Forced account creation before a one-time donation was described as a dealbreaker by participants. No sorting or filtering by urgency or funding progress. Social sharing features were missing.

The testing also validated the storytelling approach: participants didn't see "a dog" — they saw "Max, who loves sunbeams." This was an accidental finding that confirmed the narrative profile format as a core design decision to preserve.

5
My Role

As documentation lead, my work covered proposal and planning documents, requirements and specifications, the final report, presentation materials, and final submission formatting. Estimated hours: 42 expected, 55–60 actual — the increase driven by last-minute design and research changes, formatting and compliance checks, and iterative edits.

I also assisted with user interviews and user testing sessions, contributing to the research side of the project in addition to the documentation responsibilities.

Case Study 03

Intelligent Roads — Sprint Week

ARP B Sprint Week · Team Summit · 8 members including Syeda Asra Anjum · Tools: Figma, Miro · Five-day sprint format

1
The Brief

The sprint challenge was to design an innovative route optimisation solution for small and medium-sized enterprises in logistics. The focus was on two outcomes that are usually in tension: improving operational efficiency for the business and reducing carbon emissions. By the end of five days, the team aimed to have a testable prototype.

Vision: "We aim to develop the next version of a route analytics system that leverages smart business intelligence to increase revenue while reducing the ecological footprint for any SME in the sector."

2

3
Research — Day 1 Mapping

A collaborative brainstorming session mapped the logistics company's entire workflow — covering order management, tour planning, delivery execution, performance analysis, and improvement monitoring. Five main stakeholders were identified and mapped: Sales, Route Planner, Driver, Controller (Data Analyst), and Finance Manager.

From there, the team generated HMW questions across several themes: data privacy, sustainable business models, scalability, quality of automation, identifying optimisation parameters, new pattern recognition, data quality, balancing eco footprint with revenue, staff experience, user adoption of new systems, measurable targets, and usability.

The HMW questions were clustered into six focus areas that would shape the design direction: road type analysis, fuel consumption and cost, distance and travel time, eco-friendly routes, live location tracking, rest stops, and vehicle type and load.

4
Prototype — What Was Built

The Figma prototype covered eight distinct screens: an orders list view, an alternate route suggestion screen, a scheduler (single assign mode), an auto-assign screen, a route details screen, a scheduler flagged-issue state, a customer update screen, and a driver dashboard.

The prototype was built by two designers working across a single day under tight time constraints — completing user flows, screen design, and the full interactive prototype in one session. The process was described in the team report as "both mentally taxing and creatively rewarding," and provided direct insight into time-bound design execution and prioritisation under pressure.

The team worked with only two designers for the full Day 4 prototyping phase — a constraint that required rapid decision-making about what mattered most in the user experience, rather than trying to design everything.

5
Expert Interview Findings — Day 4

Expert interviews surfaced several specific, grounded insights about the logistics industry that shaped the feature scope:

— The interface needed to feel traditional and familiar to integrate smoothly into existing workflows. A radical redesign of how dispatchers work would face adoption resistance.

— AI-suggested driver/route grouping was identified as the strongest feature concept — grouping drivers and trucks in the same geographical area to reduce empty transport, which was described as a major industry-wide problem.

— Routes should not optimise solely for shortest distance. Loading and unloading times are unpredictable, particularly at locations like supermarkets, and buffer time needs to be built in.

— Once a tour has started, rescheduling mid-route is extremely difficult — the software needed to make pre-departure planning robust rather than relying on in-progress adjustments.

— The product scope is B2C (business to consumer logistics), not B2B, which affects how scheduling and routing logic should be designed.

6
Future Scope Defined

Three future scope areas were identified at the end of the sprint: self-healing routes (auto-adjusting in real time when a truck breaks down, traffic spikes, or weather disrupts a delivery — without human intervention), an AI dispatch co-pilot (a smart assistant that suggests schedule rearrangements, asks "what if" questions, and flags risks), and marketplace integration (enabling third-party shippers to list loads to fill extra truck space, turning empty transport space into revenue).

Case Study 04

Touchless Smart Bin — Inclusive Hardware Design

Digital Fabrication · Team: Khushboo Sethi, Rishikesh Sivasubramanian, Syeda Asra Anjum · Physical prototype with ultrasonic sensor

1
The Problem

Standard waste bins require a physical interaction — lifting a lid, pressing a pedal, or reaching a handle — that creates barriers for two specific groups: wheelchair users, who may not be able to reach or manipulate the lid from their seated position, and elderly users, for whom bending down is difficult or painful.

The project set out to design and build a contactless bin that neither group would need to touch or bend to use, while keeping the mechanism simple and fast enough to feel natural in everyday use.

2
Design & Technical Decisions

An ultrasonic sensor was used to detect proximity — when a person approaches, the sensor triggers the lid to open automatically, without any touch, button press, or voice command required.

The lid response time was set at 0.1 seconds. This was a deliberate decision: fast enough to feel immediate and responsive, not so fast as to trigger unintentionally from passersby.

The design was kept physically simple — a standard bin body with the sensor mounted externally — so the mechanism would be immediately recognisable and the behaviour predictable for first-time users.

An additional benefit emerged that was not the original design goal: the touchless mechanism is more hygienic for all users, not just the primary target groups. Inclusive design decisions often produce secondary benefits that serve a broader population.

3
What This Project Demonstrates

This is not a UX research project in the same sense as the others — there are no user interviews or usability testing sessions documented here. What it demonstrates is a different application of user-centred thinking: starting from the specific physical limitations of a defined user group and designing the interaction around those constraints rather than around a default able-bodied user.

The project shows that inclusive design is not a layer added on top of a product — it is a starting point that changes what the product fundamentally does and how it works.
